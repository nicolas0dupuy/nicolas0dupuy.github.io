Philosophy of my venture

I am Nicolas, the independent researcher running this reinforcement service.

I act as an external research partner in mathematics, data science, and numerical computing for academic/industrial researchers and technical/applied teams.

What I do in a few lines:

  • Backlog Clearance: I offer to take ownership of your accumulated mathematical, data science, and AI backlogs, whether you need to explore promising secondary ideas left aside or require immediate reinforcement on urgent, high-priority tasks (I’m totally domain agnostic, dealing with medical science, finance, arts, theoretical mathematics…).
  • Value-Based Milestones: We break down these tasks into project tickets together, assigning a fixed value and soft deadlines to each milestone. From there, I manage my schedule fully autonomously. We keep each other informed, but avoid formal meetings.
  • Adaptive Execution: I offer to clear tasks from basic data exploration to specialized theoretical research or numerical simulations, so to advance your backlog clearance adapted to my current cognitive state (see below about autism spectrum description).

As a researcher on the autism spectrum, I designed this personal workflow as an alternative to traditional models to fit my neurocognitive rhythm (I know it well since I am not only a researcher but also the happy autistic subject of three academic research studies). Because I love doing research and helping researchers as much as handling data (and helping data scientists), building advanced generative models (and helping more professionals!), or applying new mathematical methods (and more…), I imagined this model as risk-free for my clients, since they buy completed tasks, while I can complete challenging, high-value tasks when I have high cognitive energy, and fall back on more basic tasks otherwise.

Searching for my place in society, I was alternately a student and a home teacher on a stop-and-go basis over 20 years, and it brought together a PhD in numerical physics, a Master's (first year completed) in fundamental mathematics, distinct expertise in AI, such as generative models and reinforcement learning, web programming from a time when I wanted to be a web developer, and—at a moment when I was saturated with mathematics—Russian language at INALCO, along with self-studying about six other foreign languages to an A2/B1 level over two decades. At 46 years old, my neuroatypical wiring has led to a skillset that does not fit into standard Human Resources boxes. So I imagined this work model because I think that I can solve problems that others—even if they are much smarter than me—can't, by blending decades of graduate-level skill acquisition across multiple domains, which I would love to bring to the table.

I have set myself a five-year objective to obtain a French HDR (Habilitation à diriger les recherches). For this, I want to conduct individual numerical experiments on stochastic processes (mostly applied to finance) with generative models (such as normalizing flows), before contributing to academic research once my work gets recognition. I have been immersed in my research books for 26 years, and I want to transform my passion into something useful. My model offers a two-way benefit: your missions support my independent research path, while providing your team with early access to a scientist committed to high-demand fields and continuously developing advanced skills.

"Autism spectrum disorders (ASD) are a diverse group of conditions. They are characterized by some degree of difficulty with social interaction and communication. Other characteristics are atypical patterns of activities and behaviours, such as difficulty with transition from one activity to another, a focus on details and unusual reactions to sensations," according to the WHO, which estimates a prevalence of around 1 in 127 in the global population.

What about in academia? While there are no direct global census statistics on prevalence within academia, Simon Baron-Cohen published an article in 2001 measuring the average Autism-Spectrum Quotient across different populations. On a scale of 0 to 50, where the general population averages 16.4 (± 6.4) and the clinically diagnosed autistic population averages 35.8 (± 6.5), students in mathematics scored 21.5 (± 6.5), slightly lower than 16 UK Olympiad winners (24.5 ± 5.7).

When looking at autism at work, data from specialized organizations like the National Autistic Society and the UK Office for National Statistics (ONS) reveal that autism presents the lowest employment rate among all disability categories, with only about 21.7% of autistic adults in meaningful employment (data from 2020), just below adults with severe learning disabilities.

Yet, the autism spectrum covers a wide range of situations and intellectual levels. Dr. Neil Kenny gathered valuable testimony from academics in Irish higher education ("Tell me what my job is": A Qualitative Exploration of the Experiences of Autistic Academic Staff working in Higher Education in Ireland), and I can add mine. If you read it, here are a few themes addressed:

  1. Receiving a diagnosis lifts a significant weight: Indeed, to me, it felt like being a rabbit that landed by accident in a family of cats, finally being told that even though I love them, minor adaptations like refraining from hunting birds or wandering at night would make my life much better.
  2. Time perception is drastically different between neurodivergent and neurotypical people, and even between two autistic individuals. In the study, one participant describes being able to focus on a single task for hours without realizing time is passing, while another needs breaks more often than their colleagues. I am right in the middle of that: I loved chess competitions when I was 17 years old, with day-long games starting at 9 am that could last up to 8 hours. I attempted one of the toughest admission exams for the École Normale Supérieure (ENS); since I did not follow the traditional path, I had terrible results, but I thoroughly enjoyed the challenge. Yet, even for a task I excel at, if my mind is clouded by toxic thoughts, my perception of time is completely inverted.
  3. Routines and organisation: We all have our own routines, and organisation can be messy. To me, multitasking is like trying to juggle multiple clubs, and having even two deadlines, even very distant ones, can completely freeze me.
  4. Masking: Back to being a rabbit in the middle of the family of cats that adopted you. You love them, you want to spend time and do things with them, but they have instincts that you don't have. So you try to do the same via an unconscious copying mechanism, but your mind screams at you that it's in pain. It's often the main cause of exhaustion, leading to burnout.
  5. Sensory overload: I add it as a critical cause of exhaustion and burnout. When fatigued in crowded public spaces, repeated announcements while waiting for a train, motor backfires, or scanner beeps at the supermarket checkout force our brains to work constantly, as if we were actively processing data.
  6. More in return for our troubles: Sure, being an autistic person makes life complicated, but I wouldn't want to be any different. Participants in the study praise their hyperfocus and their drive to go deeper into their passions. I, too, would never want to lose the joy of learning logical things with such intensity.
